As well as my programming "day job" I've been involved in hiring programmers for about three years now. It's been an interesting experience, as previously my perspective had been confined to the other side of the recruitment process. Now that I've screened CV's, marked questions, reviewed coding assignments, and interviewed dozens of people, I've come to appreciate the difficulties that employers face when they're trying to fill a role, and I've think I've gained enough experience to start handing out advice, both to recruiters and to people looking for work in the industry. 

I'm going to start with some general advice for people looking for work as a software engineer. Today, I'm just going to write up my thoughts on the CV screening process. 

Understanding the CV screening process
--------------------------------------

I've noticed a tendency for candidates to think of a company as a monolithic entity, rather than a collection of diverse individuals with differing skill sets and motivations. This can lead candidates into drawing some very innacurate conclusions about what's going on when their application doesn't go as expected. 

For example, there was a [poster](https://www.reddit.com/r/javascript/comments/3qlkaj/i_was_just_rejected_via_email_by_a_recruiter/) on the [/r/javascript](http://www.reddit.com/r/javascript) subreddit recently, who'd had his CV rejected on the grounds that it "didn't contain JQuery, only Javascript" according to the response email he'd received. This was especially irritating for him, as he'd been maintaining a number of Javascript repositories on GitHub, which he'd listed on his CV. At least one made extensive use of JQuery. 

His post concluded that tech companies were clearly only interested in buzzwords on CV's, and didn't care about any kind of GitHub portfolio. He seemed to be insinuating that all the effort he'd put into the projects was essentially a waste of time.

Needless to say, this was the wrong conclusion to draw. 

Typically, the first person to see your CV does not have a technical background. They may be a member of a HR, or a specialist recruiter, and their knowledge of the world of IT can be extensive (as with ex-programmers who've changed profession), close to non-existent, or anywhere in between. People who have almost no knowledge of IT, but have to screen the CVs of programmers, almost always have to resort to check lists. By this I mean, they have a list of keywords that they look for on a CV and if those words are not present, then the CV goes in the reject pile. 

Assuming your CV gets past the first filter, the next person to look at it will probably have an engineering background. They may be a programmer or a  head of technology department, but they will understand the contents of your CV better than the people who screened it initally. *This* person will almost certainly care about your Github projects. Your CV will need to appeal to this person as well, convincing them that you are worth investing further time in. They may only have a few interview slots available per week. 

The reason the tech staff only see your CV after an initial screen is simple. Money, time, and volume. Many companies receive floods of CV's, enough to take a significant chunk out of one persons day. A developer simply does not have that much time to spend looking at CV's, and even if they did, developer time is usually considered "too expensive" by the business to squander on reading stacks of CV's. And thus, an initial screening phase is introduced, and is usally managed by whoever is coordinating the rest of the recruitment process.

Usually at this point a go or no-go decision is made about whether to give you a telephone interview, or some kind of tech test. Involving a third step in reviewing CV's is simply too cumbersome, and remember that every business is keenly aware that it is competing for the best candidates with everyone else. Having a lean recruitment process will get a business a shot at interviewing the best staff on the market before they're snapped up. 

What I want to see in a developer CV
------------------------------------

Much has been written about how to format a CV. I beleive this follows "fashions" to a certain degree, so if I spent time on it, this article would be out of date very quickly. 

Instead I'm going to focus entirely on the *content* of the CV, and what I would like to see in one:

I would like to see:

1. Who you are, and what it is you want from your next role.
2. A high level summary of your major skills. *Not* every technology you ever spent more than 10 minutes on, just the ones we could both actually have an informed conversation about. 
3. Your work and project experience, with more recent roles to the top. What was the project? What was your role in it? What technologies did you use? How long did the role last? 
4. A summary of your education, but not in excruitiating detail. No sensible person cares what grades you got at 16 if you have a BSc in Computer Science, or 20 years of development under your belt. A one line summary of the number of A-C grades before your degree (if that is applicable) is fine.
5. Links to any public resources I can look at. This can include GitHub, Bitbucket or other hosted projects, blogs, websites, lectures, videos and presentations. 

How you organise and present this data is up to you, but it needs to be easy to find, and you need to do it in 3 pages or under. 

That brings me onto the next topic; CV length. Nobody will read a 10 page CV. If your CV is longer than 3 pages, then I don't care how much experience you have, you need to get it down to 3 pages. You're going to have to be your own ruthless editor. 

I personally recommend keeping a "master" copy of your CV, with absolutely everything in it, which can be as long as you want. Then you simply "fork" off leaner copies that have been taylored for specific job applications. 

You will get it wrong, repeatedly, because we all do. Get as much feedback as you can on your CV and iteratively improve it. Pay attention to all the feedback you get, but pay most attention to the feedback that you hear repeatedly, it will guide you toward a better CV. 

In summary
----------

* Your CV has more than one audience, and you should cater to *all its audiences*. 
* Your CV will require more than one rewrite, and there should be more than one "fork" of it. 
* The CV you submit should be concise, between 2 and 3 pages. 
* If you can, you should maintain one or more repositories on GitHub that showcase what you know, and how you code. 
* Although it is heavily biased toward the US market, read [The Art Of The Developer Resume](http://www.developerdotstar.com/mag/articles/read_resume.html).
