As well as my programming "day job" I've been involved in hiring programmers for about three years now. I've screened CV's, marked questions, reviewed coding assignments, and interviewed dozens of people, I've come to appreciate the difficulties that employers face when they're trying to fill a role, and I've think I've gained enough experience to start handing out advice. 

I'm going to start with some general advice for people looking for work as a software engineer. Today, I'm just going to write up my thoughts on the CV screening process. 

Understanding the CV screening process
--------------------------------------

I've noticed a tendency for candidates to think of a company as a monolithic entity, rather than a collection of diverse individuals with differing skill sets and motivations. This can lead candidates into drawing some very innacurate conclusions about what's going on when their application doesn't go as expected. 

For example, there was a [poster](https://www.reddit.com/r/javascript/comments/3qlkaj/i_was_just_rejected_via_email_by_a_recruiter/) on the [/r/javascript](http://www.reddit.com/r/javascript) subreddit recently, who'd had his CV rejected on the grounds that it "didn't contain JQuery, only Javascript" according to the response email he'd received. This was especially irritating for him, as he'd been maintaining a number of Javascript repositories on GitHub, which he'd listed on his CV. At least one made extensive use of JQuery. 

His post concluded that tech companies were clearly only interested in buzzwords on CV's, and didn't care about any kind of GitHub portfolio. He seemed to be insinuating that all the effort he'd put into the projects was essentially a waste of time.

Needless to say, this was the wrong conclusion to draw. 

Typically, before your CV ends up on the desk with someone who has the power to interview you, it goes through a filtering process. The first person to see your CV does will probably not have a technical background. They may be a member of a HR, or a specialist recruiter. Their knowledge of the world of programming can be extensive, close to non-existent, or anywhere in between. People who have almost no knowledge of programming but have to screen the CVs of programmers, almost always have to resort to check lists. By this I mean they have a list of keywords that they look for on a CV and if those words are not present, then the CV goes in the reject pile. 

The next person to look at it will probably have an engineering background. They may be a programmer or a  head of technology department, but they will understand the contents of your CV better than the people who screened it initally. *This* person will almost certainly care about your Github projects. Your CV will need to appeal to this person as well, convincing them that you are worth investing further time in. They may only have a few interview slots available per week. 

The reason the tech staff only see your CV after an initial screen is simple. Time is money. Many companies receive floods of CV's, enough to take a significant chunk out of one persons day. A developer simply does not have that much time to spend looking at CV's. And thus, an initial screening phase is introduced, and is usally managed by whoever is coordinating the rest of the recruitment process.

Usually at this point a go or no-go decision is made about whether to give you a telephone interview, or some kind of technical test. 

What I would like to see in a CV
--------------------------------

Much has been written about how to format a CV. I'm going to ignore presentation and instead focus entirely on the *content* of the CV, and what I would like to see in one:

I would like to see:

1. Who you are, and what it is you want from your next role.
2. A high level summary of your major skills. *Not* every technology you ever spent more than 10 minutes on, just the ones we could both actually have an informed conversation about. 
3. Your work and project experience, with more recent roles to the top. What was the project? What was your role in it? What technologies did you use? How long did the role last? 
4. A summary of your education, but not in excruitiating detail. No sensible person cares what grades you got at 16 if you have a BSc in Computer Science. A one line summary of the number of A-C grades before your degree is fine.
5. Links to any public resources I can look at. This can include GitHub, Bitbucket hosted projects, blogs, websites, lectures, videos and presentations. 
How you organise and present this data is up to you, but it needs to be easy to find, and you need to do it in 3 pages or under. Nobody will read a 10 page CV. If your CV is longer than 3 pages, then I don't care how much experience you have, you need to get it down to 3 pages. 

I personally recommend keeping a "master" copy of your CV, with absolutely everything in it, which can be as long as you want. Then you simply "fork" off leaner copies that have been taylored for specific job applications. 

You will get it wrong, repeatedly, because we all do. Get as much feedback as you can on your CV and iteratively improve it. Pay attention to all the feedback you get, but pay *most* attention to the feedback that you hear repeatedly, it will guide you toward writing a better CV.
